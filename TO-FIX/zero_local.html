Did You Know?

Dorset is a county in South West England that is known for its picturesque countryside, dramatic coastline, and rich history. The county is home to the Jurassic Coast, a World Heritage Site known for its stunning cliffs, rock formations, and fossils dating back millions of years. The region's famous landmarks include Durdle Door, a natural limestone arch, and Old Harry Rocks, a series of chalk stacks located on the Isle of Purbeck.

Hampshire, on the other hand, is a county located on the southern coast of England. It is known for its stunning countryside, medieval towns, and bustling cities. Hampshire is also home to the New Forest National Park, a beautiful area of ancient woodland, heathland, and coast, which is home to a range of wildlife, including wild ponies. The county's major attractions include Winchester Cathedral, the historic city of Portsmouth, and the seaside town of Bournemouth.

The Isle of Wight is a small island located off the south coast of England. The island is known for its stunning natural beauty, diverse landscapes, and rich history. The island's major attractions include the Needles, a series of chalk stacks located on the island's western tip, and Osborne House, a former royal residence built by Queen Victoria and Prince Albert. The island is also home to a number of festivals and events, including the Isle of Wight Festival, which has been held annually since 1968 and is one of the largest music festivals in the UK.

Overall, these three regions are rich in history, natural beauty, and cultural heritage. They offer visitors a wide range of activities and attractions, from exploring ancient castles and cathedrals to enjoying scenic walks along the coastline and sampling local cuisine. Whether you're interested in history, nature, or culture, Dorset, Hampshire, and the Isle of Wight are sure to offer something to suit your interests.

Just for fun

1. In Dorset, there is a village named Piddlehinton, which is part of the Piddle Valley. Nearby, there are also villages called Piddletrenthide and Puddletown. These names have caused much amusement over the years.

2. The New Forest in Hampshire was created in 1079 as a royal hunting ground by William the Conqueror. It is home to a population of wild ponies, who are known to wander the streets of nearby towns.

3. The Isle of Wight is the only county in England to have a carnival every year, which takes place in the town of Ryde. The carnival is known for its illuminated floats, fancy dress, and music.

4. The famous Jurassic Coast in Dorset is home to some of the most incredible fossil finds in the world, including the remains of dinosaurs, prehistoric mammals, and ancient sea creatures.

5. The Isle of Wight was the inspiration for the setting of the famous novel "Swallows and Amazons" by Arthur Ransome. Many of the locations mentioned in the book, including the lake and the island itself, can still be visited today.

6. In Hampshire, there is a town called Hook, which has the unusual claim to fame of having the shortest High Street in the UK, measuring just 26 metres in length.

7. The town of Christchurch in Dorset is home to the smallest church in England, known as the "Old Church of St. Nicholas". It has just six pews and can only seat 12 people at a time.

8. The Isle of Wight is home to the world-famous music festival, which has been held annually since 1968. The festival has attracted some of the biggest names in music over the years, including Bob Dylan, Jimi Hendrix, and The Rolling Stones.

9. Hampshire is home to the UK's oldest vineyard, which was established in 1952. The vineyard produces a range of award-winning wines, including sparkling wines, red wines, and rosés.

10. The Dorset town of Shaftesbury is famous for its steep cobbled street known as Gold Hill, which featured in a famous Hovis bread advert in the 1970s. The advert has become a beloved part of British pop culture and the hill is a popular tourist attraction to this day.
